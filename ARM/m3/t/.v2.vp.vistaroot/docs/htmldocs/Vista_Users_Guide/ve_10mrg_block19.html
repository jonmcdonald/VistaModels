Autorouting Channels

Autorouting is automatic in that wherever possible, it avoids cutting across existing elements. Automatically-routed lines look the same as manually-routed lines. In addition, they can be adjusted manually after you create them automatically.


  Note

There are no special automatic-routing icons for bidirectional channels. After automatically routing a channel, you can change its pins or connectors to bidirectional by editing their attributes.

To autoroute a channel —

  1. Click the appropriate icon.
  1. Click where you want the channel to start. To start from a block, click inside the block, near the position on the edge where you want to start.
    To start at a pin of a component (see "Instantiated Components"), click the pin, or inside the component near the pin. If you start a new channel from a component pin, the signal automatically takes the name and contents of the pin, unless there is a conflict of names with existing objects in the same diagram.

  1. Move the mouse to where you want the channel to end.

Along the way, you may click wherever you want a corner. But even if you create a direct diagonal connection cutting across several elements, corners are created for you automatically, as necessary.

If the new channel that you are drawing crosses another channel, they do not intersect unless you click the channel that you cross.

  • To end on a block, click inside the block near the position on the edge where you want to end.
  • To end at any point on a channel, click that point.
  • To end on a pin of a component, click the pin, or inside the component near the pin. If you start a new channel in empty space and end it on a component pin, the channel automatically takes the name and contents of the pin, unless there is a conflict of names with existing objects in the same diagram.
  • To end elsewhere, unconnected, double-click where you want to end.

A word about style. Depending on the complexity of the block diagram that you are creating, or even on your own graphic preference, there are two different ways of achieving connectivity between different elements in a block diagram:

  • You can extend complete channels from, for example, one block to another, and in this way graphically depict the source and destination of a given channel.
  • Because Vista makes a logical connection between two channels having an identical name, you have the option of representing channels as discontinuous lines, as long as they are assigned the same name, of course. For more information on using this second possibility, see "Discontinuous Channels".
